Chapter 3

GRPO: optimizing a verifiable reward

Group Relative Policy Optimization takes the opposite tack from DPO. Nobody hands it good and bad answers. The model generates its own answers, a rule scores each one, and the model shifts toward the answers that scored above their peers. When the reward is something you can check by rule — like whether a yes/no/maybe verdict is correct — this is a clean, cheat-resistant signal.

The loop

For each prompt the model produces a small group of answers. Each is scored; an answer that beats its group's average gets reinforced, one below average gets suppressed. Averaging within the group is the trick that removes the need for a separate value network.

The reward is just a rule

No neural reward model, no human labels at training time — the reward is a few lines that read the answer and check it against the known verdict. The verifiable part (does the verdict match?) carries the signal; a small format bonus keeps answers verdict-first.

# PubMedQA gives us a checkable answer, so the reward can be a rule:
reward = 1.0  if verdict(answer) == reference_verdict   # the real signal
       + 0.2  if answer starts with a verdict at all     # light shaping

The 240 training prompts are deliberately balanced — 80 yes, 80 no, 80 maybe — so the model can't farm reward by always guessing the majority class.

Two things hold it flat. First, the KL leash: GRPO is penalized for drifting from the SFT reference, and that penalty stayed near 0.001 the whole run — the policy barely moved. Second, the budget: 60 steps of four prompts is a small amount of signal for a 7B model. So a flat reward here means "didn't move far," which is by design as much as it is a limit — Chapter 4 shows exactly how little the verdicts changed.

Why bf16 LoRA and not 4-bit here? GRPO generates answers during training, and in this stack the generation step trips over 4-bit quantized weights. So the GRPO policy runs in bf16 (still a small rank-16 adapter on a frozen base, still one 24 GB GPU). DPO, which never generates, kept the 4-bit QLoRA setup. A small asymmetry worth naming.
